ABOUT

Manifold is an open-ended collective that evolves around a common position of care and sustenance . From a background in architecture,we take action to think-and work-with what is around us in both its material and immaterial capacities. Dealing with what is already there makes sense to us in times of exhaustion, but also, and maybe more, it builds with elements of meaning.The context we work with, is a product of existing and previous knowledges to learn from and think with. This is not a position of conservatism, rather we see it as a most fertile basis for something-anything new .

Convivial Construction
1/7
2/7

Our concept of the circular economy is anthropocentric, aiming to “keep products, materials and components at their highest value at all times” (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019) without questioning the value system we are conforming to. When matter is no longer useful for humans it is degraded, it is called “leakage”, with no consideration for the possible benefit or opportunity to other ecological cycles within our material flow. This project makes a productive critique of the ‘circular economy’ by proposing an alternative material culture that recognises the built environment as an extension of the wider ecosystem and social (cultural, economic, political) content. The role of the designer shifts to work between the architecture, and the landscape that produces and is produced by that architecture. This demands a technical and locally-specific understanding of the time-scale and availability of materials, and the ecological conditions they affect outside of our human frame of reference. I propose this as a Convivial Construction practice - where conviviality is the building of of long-lasting, engaging and open ended relationships with non-humans and ecologies (Büscher & Fletcher, 2019).

Architectures of Everyday Activism
1/6
2/6

The existence of Lützerath is threatened by the enlargement of an open-pit coal mine. To avoid the expansion of this extractive landscape, the anarcho-environmentalist movement Lützerath lebt (Lützerath lives) occupies the remains of the village and surrounding forest. Through the construction of treehouses, blockades and the squatting of abandoned buildings, the activists are settled in the endangered area as a non-hierarchical and self organized community, physically stopping the arrival of excavators that would mean its destruction. The antagonism between the neighboring realities the mine and the occupation is documented in the short film Architecture of Everyday Activism and reasserts the value of the activist movements use of architecture in challenging the extraction of natural resources by living on top of those.

Space of Coexistence
1/7
2/7

It is in the public spaces of the city, where the collective of society coexists. Here the businessman, the worker, and the family use the same spaces to gather, dance, eat and play. It is a space meant for all, a, what the city of Rotterdam calls “urban living room” in the city. But not all parts of society are accepted here in the same way. Some groups - in particular the homeless, are hindered from using these places. Being the “other” in the city, the unwanted, the outcasts, they are not welcome and the authorities take active actions against their presence. This takes place either through policing and surveillance or through hostile design. Paradoxically this group of people hindered from using the space are the ones who depend on it the most, relying on open spaces as a home and dwelling space. The project “spaces of coexistence” - attempts to create an inclusive space, where society and the “other” of the homeless can coexist in an architectural composition of public spaces where a museum can turn into a shelter or a dance studio into a food court. It embraces the differences in society and their coexistence in the city.

Demolition or (Re- )Construction
1/6
2/6

Demolition or (de)construction was an exhibition at the break of an involuntary letting go. In the week after, the last inhabitants (that was us, too) left Chemistry. It was our last chance to open its doors before the demolishers would come in. It tried to show anyone involved with or interested in this place what was about to disappear. That means not just the concrete, bricks and gardens, but with it the latent potential for things new this place was still beaming with. It also was our chance to reach out beyond the academic refrain of the project, inviting neighbours, former chemists and others. In the old library we set out 1:33 fragment models at eye-height that described both the typical and exceptional moments in the building. Around it, four ‘lenses’ (after Guattari’s three ecologies) deconstructed our immersed view of the capacities these spaces still held. Above it, the empty bookshelves were a looming reminder of time and end.

Gele Scheikunde
1/10
2/10

‘Gele Scheikunde’ is the former chemistry faculty in Delft we were living in at the time of this graduation project. It had been sitting empty for some years and grew into some kind of inner-city fringe. From our immersed view we have tried to build an alternative perspective that reached from the urban renewal plans demolishing the building, via our own and our neighbours’ experiences into a speculative but realistic future it could have had. A variety of documents reframe what the developers had labeled obsolete before us. We aimed to transvaluate those same material conditions by always keeping them in their not-obsolete networks of use and meaning. Without ever having serious hope of actually influencing Chemistry’s looming disappearance, we could make claims with some weight and seriousness as they were derived directly from an unconventional but active and flourishing dwelling situation. In the end it produced a strategy of minimal interventions spread out over time, securing the temporal occupation we were part of as the ground for further but gradual development of the site. Small alterations to the building could make it accessible to more dwellers, while some bigger changes would also allow few public functions. By giving due attention to the peripheral requirements of long-term development, we tried to give the project the time and space in needs to benefit from the capacity of the building. It is about facilitating that use and material grow together into some more and new, rooted and reliant on each other.

The Black Shed
1/6

The Black Shed is a project to refurbish an existing rural workshop space into a low-cost, environmentally-sustainable, self-build home in the Lake District National Park. The shed is located at Lawson Park, the base of Grizedale Arts rural residential arts programme, and will provide additional, self-contained and adaptable accommodation for curators and artists with dependants or access needs. The process of refurbishment will be approached as educational opportunity: it will be built by local women through a series of workshops, addressing the gender imbalance and accessibility of the construction industry through teaching craft and construction skills. It will also provide a demonstrator project for creative practices of self-building and sustainable development in the local area, responding to the lack of affordable and accessible housing for young people in the National Park. The project to date has involved a practical research phase, a series of design and construction workshops that fed into a design proposal, currently at planning stage. Project in partnership with Grizedale Arts, in collaboration with Karina Armanda (Kuznecova) and Choon Yuan Wang. With support from Foyle Foundation, Great Place, Lakes and Dales, and Arts Council England.

Found and Foraged Buildling Systems
1/5
2/5

What can we build with? A 5-week residency with Grizedale Arts at The Farmers Arms followed a process of unlearning my expectations of what materials have a place in contemporary construction. Working closely with the resident chef in the pub-kitchen during this time, my practice responded to the way I observed her exploring the (edible) potential of a landscape by being present in the landscape, touching, tasting and interacting with it in a curious and caring way. Using a tactile, hands-on approach to rediscover the available material landscape of the Lake District and its potential construction properties, I developed a series of prototypes for Wattle-and-Daub wall panels - a system designed to adapt a vernacular way of building to contemporary construction culture. My ‘recipes' reworked traditional Daub mixtures to work with found, foraged and waste material from the local landscape and by-products of the pub. eg. could used chip oil add extra binding where soil clay-content is low? Could weaving invasive bracken give wattle surface? The residency culminated in an open workshop session on Wattle and Daub construction at the Lowick Village Show 2022. The Farmers Arms is a historic public house on the edge of the Lake District, currently being developed by local arts organisation Grizedale Arts around a focus of craft, community and experimental production, to rethink the role of the pub in contemporary British culture.
